Advocacy is necessary because our social identities are tied to systems of power. The chapter builds on three key takeaways:

  • #1: Difference Matters: Social identities (race, gender, class, disability, etc.) aren't just labels; they persistently matter because society has built hierarchies around them.
  • #2: Power Counts: Powerful systems (media, politics, etc.) create and maintain these hierarchies. We must understand power to challenge it.
  • #3: Communication Rules: Communication is the tool used to build inequality, but it's also our most powerful tool to dismantle it.

üß† Key Concepts for Advocates

To be an effective advocate, you must first "Be Mindful" by recognizing these key concepts in yourself and others.

T.U.I. (Thinking Under the Influence)

This is the chapter's most important term. It means thinking under the influence of "prejudices, stereotypes, and dominant ideologies."

  • Example: Assuming someone in a wheelchair has a "gloomy outlook" or that someone who doesn't speak "proper" English is "unintelligent."
  • Advocacy Step: Mindfulness is the first step. You must catch your own T.U.I. moments before you can proactively challenge them in the world.

Intent vs. Impact

This is the most common conflict in communication about identity.

Intent = What you meant to do.

Impact = The reality of what your words/actions did.

An advocate knows that "I didn't mean it" is not an excuse. It dismisses the harm caused. A proactive response is to listen to the impact, apologize, and learn.

üõ†Ô∏è How to Be an Advocate: "Be Proactive"

Advocacy is about being proactive: "taking the initiative and the responsibility to make things happen." It's the opposite of being a silent bystander.

1. Give Descriptive Feedback

This is a crucial skill for challenging power without making the other person defensive. Don't accuse; describe.

  • Accusatory (Bad): "You're racist! You always ignore me."
  • Descriptive (Good): "I've noticed that in the last two meetings, you haven't called on me when I raised my hand. I feel ignored and unvalued."

This focuses on the behavior and its impact, which opens the door for dialogue and change.

2. Check Your Privilege

Citing Ijeoma Oluo, this means "to pause and consider how your advantages... are keeping you from fully understanding the struggles" of others. You can use your privilege to challenge the system (e.g., a cisgender person advocating for transgender rights).

3. Challenge In-Groups & Power Dynamics

Advocacy starts in your own circles.

  • In-Groups: Proactively challenge "we/us" vs. "they/them" language or discriminatory jokes (e.g., "I don't think that's funny").
  • Power Dynamics: Use your voice in unequal relationships. (e.g., The author questioning their doctor's recommendation by bringing in their own research).

4. Learn About Other Cultures

Don't wait to be taught. Proactively learn about other social identity groups. The author gives a personal example of being critiqued for not considering neurodiversity, which prompted them to do their own research and revise their work.

  2. In what ways can understanding your own identities help you to improve communication?
  3. How has race, gender, or class influenced how your identity is perceived by others?
  4. What is one practical way you think you could use to advocate for inclusion in a community or classroom?
  5. When have you seen advocacy done really well, like on campus or a community, and what do you think made it effective?
  6. When do you feel the most aware of yourself and who you are and how does communication play a role in that?
  7. What's one issue or group that you have felt compelled to advocate for and why?
  8. What's one cause you would advocate about related to your personal identity and how?
  9. How can diverse perspectives make advocacy movements stronger?
  10. Do you think learning about social identity will make you think differently about how you communicate with others and how?
